The Lead Story

A CRATE, SEALED; A BOTTLE, CERTAIN.

Every crate from this house holds exactly one bottle, drawn from the standing inventory and appraised between $110 and $180. No jackpots. No empties. Just wine, and the paperwork to prove it.

By The Cellar Desk  ·  Filed from the bond

CALIFORNIA, IN BOND — Each bottle in the inventory rests where it has always rested: in a bonded warehouse, at fifty-five degrees, under seventy percent humidity, its condition attested by instruments that do not sleep. When a crate is opened, the bottle it names does not move. What changes hands is the right to it — to sell it back at once for store credit at appraised value, to keep it sleeping under our roof, or to have it driven, signed for, and set on a Californian table.

No Empty Crates

Skeptics will ask what stands behind the stencil. The answer is unglamorous and complete: continuous custody, storage attestation on the hour, and an authentication file older than the sale. Every crate holds one bottle. Every bottle holds its ground. The rest — the machinery of record-keeping — is our business, conducted quietly, so that yours can be the wine. The presses, and the cellar doors, are now open.

THE THREE DISPOSITIONS

What may lawfully become of a revealed bottle

Disposition the First

Sell it back, on the spot

The house buys at appraised value, instantly, in store credit. No listing, no waiting, no haggling in the classifieds. The bottle returns to the standing inventory; the credit returns to you, spendable on the next crate.

Disposition the Second

Keep it in the cellar

The bottle stays exactly where it has aged best: our bond, our racks, our thermometers. Storage is free for ninety days, then 2.5% a year — roughly the price of worrying about it yourself, without the worrying.

Disposition the Third

Ship it home

California addresses only, an adult signature at the door, and a bottle that has never known a warm afternoon. It travels the way it was stored: on its side, in the dark, taken seriously the whole way.
